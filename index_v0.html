Research

Due to the inherent complexity of reconstructing the past of living organisms, as well as to biologists' varied scientific inclinations, evolutionary inference is usually carried out at either micro- or macroevolutionary scales.

In my research, I look for ways to better understand and bridge the gap between these two scales of inquiry, while tackling questions of broad interest in evolutionary biology. My work aligns with the idea behind phylogeography "sensu Avise". I develop, implement and use general evolutionary models in an attempt to conduct statistical inference at distinct temporal scales simultaneously. Below, I outline some of the topics and questions I am currently (or am interested in) addressing.

INTEGRATIVE MODELS IN EVOLUTIONARY INFERENCE

With the development of faster computers and statistical tools over the past decades, it is now possible to use integrative models that draw information from multiple different sources of data simultaneously, through joint parameter estimation. Integrative models make it possible for some kinds of data to contribute signal to the estimation of parameters they would not normally inform (under separate evolutionary models), as well as to add their signal to that of other data partitions.

I am one of the developers of BEAST2, a package where integrative models are implemented in a Bayesian framework. Bayesian statistics provides us with a convenient set-up for the construction of such models. My research involves the refinement of some of BEAST's model components in addition to the development of new submodel pieces. The goal is to enable evolutionary inferences that draw information from discrete and continuous morphological characters (including fossils), molecular data, and species geographical distributions. The difficulty lies in having all these parts talking to one another coherently (and fast enough!).

TRAIT EVOLUTION

Evolutionary biologists are not only interested in how new species come to be and how they are related, but also why some species they look the way they look. Traits can vary immensely among species, and common investigations include determining whether two traits are correlated, if the evolutionary rate of a trait varies across clades or environments, and whether certain traits make speciation more likely.

HISTORICAL BIOGEOGRAPHY & SPECIATION

With the development of faster computers and statistical tools over the past decades, it is now possible to use models that can simultaneously draw information from multiple different sources of data. These integrative models allow for joint, robust estimation of many parameters of interest, which can in turn reveal interesting aspects of the evolutionary process. This is a place where Bayesian inference provides us with a framework to construct and perform inference under such integrative models.

I am one of the developers of BEAST2, a package where such an integrative model is implemented in a Bayesian framework. My research involves the refinement of some of BEAST's model components (which lack in realism) in addition to the development of new submodel pieces. The goal is to enable evolutionary inferences that draw information from discrete and continuous morphological characters (including fossils), molecular data, and species geographical distributions. The difficulty lies in having all these parts talking to one another coherently (and fast enough!).
